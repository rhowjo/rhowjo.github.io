Task – Collaborative Discussion I – Initial Post

The aim of this post will be to evaluate the practices of Corazón in comparison to the British Computer Society Code of Conduct (BCS Code of conduct, 2022) regarding the (harmless) security vulnerability discovered by a researcher in their medical device (Case: Medical implant risk analysis - ACM ethics 2021).

There are 4 parts to the code of conduct, and each will be considered in turn, with reference to specific codes.

Public Interest:

By the very nature of the implant device, Corazón complies with this part of the code as it demonstrates due regard for public health and wellbeing (1.a). Additionally, by having an accessible device that has an easy-to-use app, Corazon also promotes equal access to the benefits of IT (1.d).

Professional Competence and Integrity:

Instead of using proprietary techniques, using standard cryptographic algorithms demonstrates compliance with 2.a, only undertaking work within professional competence.

Duty to Relevant Authority:

By actively pursuing regulatory compliance to regulatory standards, Corazón demonstrates its due diligence to fulfilling obligations to the relevant regulations.

Duty to the Profession:

With on-going commitments to consult with researchers and openness of product performance and security by welcoming scrutiny, Corazón proves that it complies with all aspects of this code.

On this basis, Corazon shows exemplary practices regarding ethics. However, in lieu of examining this case in isolation, there is a call for greater regulatory frameworks as not all risks are accounted for in current standards for smart implant devices (Harmon et al., 2015) thus it remains to be seen whether this case would uphold the ethics if new regulations were put in place.

Task – Reasoning Quiz

This quiz sought to test knowledge of differentiating between deductive reasoning and inductive reasoning. Deductive reasoning is making an inference based on accepted facts or premise and for it to work, the premise must be true (Inductive vs. deductive vs. Abductive Reasoning). E.g. All humans have hearts. Rhiannon is a human. Thus, she must have a heart. On the other hand, for inductive reasoning, the premise is likely but not necessarily true based on observations (Hawthorne, 2018).

References


Hawthorne, J. (2018) Inductive logic, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. Available at: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-inductive/ (Accessed: 03 January 2024).

Inductive vs. deductive vs. Abductive Reasoning (no date) Merriam-Webster. Available at: https://www.merriam-webster.com/grammar/deduction-vs-induction-vs-abduction (Accessed: 03 January 2023).

Task – Ethics in Computing

This is a short review of the potential ethical issues that may be associated with the role of a software engineer within a recruitment company such as Adecco. This is not an exhaustive list of ethical and legal issues surrounding the role and I will examine such issues briefly.

One of the largest potential concerns is the collection of PII from candidates. This data would be needed for contacting clients via telephone and email, and as such, would need to be stored securely and wherever possible, be anonymised and keyed if data is to be used outside of normal communications for a legitimate purpose. Thus, it is important to be aware of regulations such as GDPR that govern how such data should be processed and maintained (Regulation - 2016/679 - en - GDPR - EUR-lex 2016).

Furthermore, should a candidate wish to delete their data, the software engineer should be aware of the process to remove all PII where applicable.

Task - Literature review Outline

I chose to conduct my literature review on the topic of the use of artificial intelligence in medical diagnosis.

The intention of this literature review is to establish the applications of artificial intelligence in undertaking a medical diagnosis. Historically, AI has been used as early as the 1970s for medical purposes. Due to the exponential growth of AI in recent years and the sensitive morality of medical diagnosis it would be prudent to explore the history of AI in healthcare settings to better understand the applications, ethics, and bias in this context.

Through an initial evaluation of the topic, three common themes emerge around the topic of artificial intelligence applications in medical diagnostics. The history and development of AI in medical diagnostics, the applications and efficacy of AI in medical diagnostics, and the potential bias in AI and medical diagnostics. This literature review intends to establish and cross reference several sources under each topic, with the aim to provide an overview of the prevailing themes within each of the three aforementioned topics.

Within history and development of AI in medical diagnostics, this literature review will evaluate studies from the infancy of computer technology to trace the lineage of the modern-day AI applications. This segues into discussing the second topic of applications and efficacy in AI. Through this second section, the literature review intends to analyse the different methodologies of AI, for example, the use of deep learning to detect COVID-19 during the most recent pandemic. Through the study of multiple applications, the literature review will finally discuss the risk of bias in AI medical diagnostics. Through assessing multiple applications in the previous section, the literature review will then assess the many ways in which bias can factor into AI models.

Finally, the literature review will discuss the limitations, strengths, and discrepancies. These are expected to include the limited scope of the review, the breadth of the study in encompassing multiple areas of a broad topic, and the limitation of effective documentation of bias in academic literature.

Task – Inappropriate Use of Surveys

In 2018, there was a scandal relating to the sharing of data obtained from Facebook users obtained via surveys. Cambridge Analytica, the company responsible for the scandal, sold data that was used to create psychological profiles of voters for political campaigns (Confessore, 2018).

The data was obtained by an app that paid users to take a personality quiz. To complete the quiz, users were required to consent to give access to their Facebook profiles and their friends profiles (Cambridge Analytica, 2018).

The owner of the app then agreed to give the data to Cambridge Analytica which then used it to combine with other sources to create personality profiles. The aim was to use this data to identify personalities of voters and influence their behaviour (Prokop, 2018).

This is not the only time that personality quizzes have been used. Presenting as innocuous, data from quizzes produced by CubeYou have been used to help marketers find customers – a very similar case to the one discussed above, except that instead of trying to influence behaviour of voters, it’s customers (Mishcastillo, 2018).

In both cases, there is a clear misuse of data gained from surveys and is unquestionably unethical. Users are partaking in surveys for monetary gain and/or enjoyment to find out what personality type they have. Whilst there was consent given for profile details, there is little regulation on what can happen to the data gathered regarding its sale and further use.

Task - Collaborative Discussion II Initial Post

There are ethical concerns over the courses of action that Abi can take regarding the presentation of the data.

First and foremost, it is important to note that Abi is not obligated to do anything in a particular way and can present the facts in whatever manner he chooses. However, the best practice would be to present all the facts, regardless of which conclusion it supports and should provide a holistic view of the data to the manufacturer. It is important to recognise the ability of a data professional to misuse statistical tools (Thiese et al., 2015) and therefore it is crucial that Abi ensures that he does not invalidate results in this way.

Beyond this, Abi has no responsibility for how the data is then used. But, in the case of food manufacturing, there are rules around nutritional values and food standards such as Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 (Lex - 02011R1169-20180101 - en - EUR-Lex 2011), so the consequences of the outcome of the project are limited in scope.
